# Writing Principles

Writing transfers something from your mind into your readers mind. Every choice either helps or hinders that transfer.

## Know your reader

Once you understand something you forget what it felt like not to understand it. Your jargon and assumptions and logical leaps feel obvious to you but form invisible walls for readers.

Picture your specific audience before you begin. When uncertain about expertise level define terms within dependent clauses so experts can skip past while novices learn without feeling patronized. Example: using heuristics, or mental shortcuts, to solve the problem.

Assume readers know nothing about your specific topic but are intelligent enough to follow clear reasoning. Lead them step by step through the same sequence of ideas that made the subject clear to you.

## Choose simple words

Never use a long word where a short one will do. Prefer help over assistance, many over numerous, ease over facilitate, rest over remainder. Saxon roots often beat Latin derivatives.

Never use jargon or technical language when everyday English works. Foreign phrases, scientific terms, and specialized vocabulary exclude readers who could otherwise understand you.

Never use a worn out metaphor or figure of speech. Phrases like ring the changes, Achilles heel, swan song, and hotbed have lost their power through overuse. They signal that you are not thinking freshly.

## Cut ruthlessly

If it is possible to cut a word out, cut it out. Clutter weakens prose. Readers have short attention spans and every unnecessary word taxes them.

Beware phrases that add nothing: it should be pointed out that, it is interesting to note, the fact that, in terms of, at this point in time. Replace them with nothing or with simpler alternatives.

Up to half of most first drafts can be cut. Prune ruthlessly. If you are keeping something just because you like that you wrote it, that is a sign it should go.

## Use active constructions

Prefer active voice over passive. The active voice is direct and vigorous. The committee approved the plan beats The plan was approved by the committee.

Make central characters the subjects of your sentences. Use verbs to name the actions those characters perform. Readers follow prose more easily when they can see who is doing what.

Avoid nominalizations, which turn verbs into nouns. Decide is clearer than make a decision. Investigate beats conduct an investigation. Verbs create momentum; nouns create sludge.

## Put statements in positive form

Say what something is rather than what it is not. He usually arrived late is stronger than He was not often on time. Positive statements are easier to process and remember.

Avoid hedging and over qualifying. Phrases like it could perhaps be argued that, it might be the case that, or to some extent signal uncertainty and weaken your authority. If you have something to say, say it.

## Move between abstract and concrete

Abstractions make claims. Concrete details make those claims believable and memorable. Neither works alone.

Lead with the claim then support it with specific detail then connect back to the larger point. Do not pile examples hoping readers will infer your argument. Do not stack abstractions hoping readers will supply evidence.

Reduce abstract principles to images readers can visualize. Concrete language processes faster than abstract language. Readers connect with people and specific situations, not with abstractions like profitability or utilization.

## Structure for clarity

Make the paragraph the unit of composition. Each paragraph should develop one idea. Begin with a topic sentence that states that idea.

Keep related words together. The subject and verb of a sentence should not be separated by long interrupting phrases. Place modifiers close to the words they modify.

Put emphatic material at the end. The final position in a sentence carries the most weight. Build toward your point rather than burying it in the middle.

Express parallel ideas in parallel form. Lists should use consistent grammatical structure. Inconsistent structure forces readers to work harder.

## Preserve rhythm

Language has music. Some words carry emotional weight or pacing even when logically unnecessary. He simply walked away contains a logically useless adverb that suggests ease or dismissal.

Read sentences aloud. You will hear awkward rhythms, monotonous patterns, and sentences that all sound the same length. Vary your sentence structure. Mix short sentences with longer ones.

The goal is prose that sounds like a person talking, not a robot processing text.

## Match tone to context

Casual conversation allows fragments and contractions. Formal documents require complete sentences and measured pace. Technical specifications demand precision over personality. Persuasive writing allows more rhythm and imagery.

Read your prose aloud. If it sounds robotic add warmth. If it sounds chatty where gravity is needed tighten. The goal is appropriate register not uniform style.

Be yourself when you write. Readers connect with a human voice, not with institutional language. A simple style is the result of hard work and hard thinking; a muddled style reflects a muddled thinker.

## Avoid overformatting

Bullets and headers and bold text are organizational tools not substitutes for clear thinking. Excessive formatting makes prose feel like a slide deck.

Most explanations and reports and documents should flow as paragraphs with minimal structural interruption. Use lists only when items genuinely need to be enumerated. Use headers only when sections are long enough to require navigation.

## Rewrite

Writing is rewriting. Nobody gets it right the first time. The process of revision is where clarity emerges.

Ask yourself constantly: what am I trying to say? Then look at what you have written and ask: have I said it? Is there anything I can cut? Is there a simpler way to say this?

Good writing does not come naturally. It requires hard work, practice, and the willingness to throw out what does not serve the reader.

## Break rules when necessary

Break any of these principles sooner than write something awkward or unnatural. Rules are tools, not laws. The ultimate test is whether your reader understands you clearly and keeps reading willingly.

The goal is not to follow rules but to serve readers. If a rule gets in the way of clarity or grace, set it aside.
